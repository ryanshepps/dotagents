# Curse of Knowledge

> Writers know what they're writing about. Readers don't. The single biggest source of bad prose is forgetting what the reader doesn't yet know.

## Why

Steven Pinker calls this "the single best explanation I know of why good people write bad prose." The writer has internalized vocabulary, context, mental models, and assumptions. The reader hasn't. When the writer forgets the gap, prose goes opaque.

This is *not* about dumbing things down. It's about modeling the reader. Assume they're as smart as you. Don't assume they've read what you've read or thought what you've thought.

## How it shows up

1. **Unexplained jargon** — "the check valve gets sucked into the engine" reads fine to a mechanic, opaque to most blog readers. Either explain or rephrase.
2. **Acronyms with no expansion** — first appearance always spelled out.
3. **Pronouns without antecedents** — "this" referring back to a fuzzy concept three paragraphs ago.
4. **Skipped logical steps** — writer mentally bridged A → C; reader needs B written down.
5. **Assumed shared context** — "as we all know," "obviously," "of course" are usually warning signs that the writer didn't bother to make it obvious.
6. **Insider vocabulary** — terms of art that signal in-group membership instead of communicating.

## The fix: cold reader test

Before publishing, imagine handing the piece to a smart friend who has never thought about this topic. Read it through their eyes. Where would they stop and say "wait, what"? Those spots get one of:

- Define the term (briefly, in-line)
- Show an example
- Add the missing logical step
- Cut the unnecessary technical detail entirely

## Pattern

Three good moves when introducing specialized content:

1. **Explain in passing** — "the check valve (a little orange flap that controls airflow inside the engine)" — parenthetical, doesn't slow the prose
2. **Analogize** — compare the unknown thing to something the reader knows
3. **Show before naming** — describe what the thing does first, then give the term

## Examples

**Cursed:**
> The PCV system on the Gen 1 LUJ failed because the IMV oneway flap delaminated under crankcase pressure.

**Decursed:**
> The engine has a little orange flap on the intake manifold — a check valve. Its job is to keep airflow moving in one direction. On these engines, the flap eventually breaks off and gets sucked into the engine. Now there's an air leak, the fuel mixture is wrong, and the car runs rough.

Same content. The second version teaches as it goes.

## In critique mode

Read as the cold reader. Flag every term, acronym, pronoun, or logical leap that lost you. Don't suggest the rewrite — mark the gap. The author knows the content; you don't. That's exactly the diagnostic.

## When jargon is right

- **Audience is technical** — writing for mechanics, no need to define check valve.
- **Term is the topic** — sometimes the whole post is about the term; defining it is the point.
- **Voice depends on it** — if the writer's voice is plainly insider-coded (niche subculture blog), spelling everything out kills voice. Match the audience.

Default to explain. Skip when audience is provably technical.
