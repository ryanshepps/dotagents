# Write Consistently

> Writing improves through volume, not insight. Daily practice on real surfaces — blogs, journals, public posts — builds skill that no single tip teaches.

## Why

Every craft rule in this knowledge base — cut clutter, lead with the news, avoid hedging — is faster to read than to internalize. You internalize them by writing, getting things wrong, noticing, and writing again. There is no shortcut. Reading 100 essays on prose without writing teaches less than writing 100 bad drafts.

Volume also protects against the perfectionism trap: you can't agonize over a single sentence for an hour if you've committed to publishing tomorrow. Deadlines force decisions; decisions train taste.

## The shape of practice

What works, roughly in priority:

1. **A public surface with stakes.** Blog, newsletter, internal memos, even tweets. Strangers' eyeballs raise the floor on quality more than any private journal will.
2. **A regular cadence.** Weekly is the minimum that builds skill; daily is faster. Irregular bursts mostly waste the warmup.
3. **Variety of forms.** Short and long, argumentative and narrative, technical and personal. Skills transfer across genres only after you've practiced each.
4. **A few re-reads.** Reading your own work cold (a week later) is the most efficient feedback loop you have. You'll see what bored you that you couldn't see fresh.

## The hard part

The hard part isn't the writing — it's the publishing. Writing without publishing lets you optimize for self-flattery; the unfinished draft is always going to be brilliant once you "finish" it. Publishing forces a real version into the world and gives you something to be embarrassed by next year, which is the actual marker of growth.

## When NOT to push volume

- **Burnout.** If writing has become joyless, take time off rather than grinding.
- **Without revision.** Volume without re-reading produces volume, not skill. The loop is *write → publish → re-read later → notice → write better*.
- **As a substitute for reading.** Writers who only write and don't read flatten quickly. Inputs feed outputs.

## In coaching mode

When asked "how do I get better at writing," the honest answer is almost never another rule. It's: *write more, on a regular schedule, in public.* Resist the urge to give a tip. The bottleneck is volume.
