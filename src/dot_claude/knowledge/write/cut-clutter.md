# Cut Clutter

> Every word earns its place or it dies. Strip filler, hedge words, inflated phrases, and circular constructions until each sentence does real work.

## Why

Clutter is the disease of writing. Every padding word steals attention from the words that matter. The reader's eye glides past noise — they remember whatever cuts through. Brief sentences hit harder. Most drafts can lose 20-30% of their words and gain clarity.

This is the central rule from Zinsser's *On Writing Well*: re-examine each sentence and ask whether every word is doing new work. If not, cut it.

## Hit list

Words and phrases that almost always pad without adding meaning:

- **Hedges**: just, really, very, basically, actually, simply, quite, literally, somewhat, rather
- **Throat-clearers**: in order to → to · at this point in time → now · due to the fact that → because · for the purpose of → for · in the event that → if · with regard to → about
- **Doubled-up phrases**: each and every, first and foremost, end result, completely eliminate, advance planning, future plans, past history
- **Empty intensifiers**: very unique, totally destroyed, completely full, absolutely essential
- **It-is/There-are openers**: "It is important to note that X" → "X" · "There are many reasons why we should X" → "We should X because..."
- **Nominalizations**: "make a decision" → "decide" · "conduct an investigation" → "investigate" · "give consideration to" → "consider"

## Pattern

Read each sentence twice:

1. **First read**: does the sentence's *meaning* require this word?
2. **Second read**: would a smart reader miss anything if it were gone?

If both no, cut.

## Examples

| Cluttered | Cut |
|-----------|-----|
| In order to fix the leak, I really had to first find it. | To fix the leak, I had to find it. |
| It is important to note that the bolt actually snapped. | The bolt snapped. |
| At the end of the day, what I basically learned was patience. | I learned patience. |
| There are a number of different reasons why this happened. | This happened for several reasons. |
| The end result was that I had to make a decision about whether to continue. | I had to decide whether to continue. |

## In critique mode

Highlight padding words inline. Don't rewrite the sentence — mark the cuts and let the author decide. Cluster them: scattered "every word matters" notes get tedious, but a list of 10 hedge words flagged together gets through.

## When clutter is right

- **Voice quirks** that ARE the voice — `Anyway,` `So,` `I dunno,` — these are choices, not clutter. Don't strip them.
- **Pacing pauses** — a deliberately wordy sentence to slow the reader before a punchy fragment. Read aloud to tell the difference.
- **Hedging when the claim deserves it** — "I think" before a contested opinion is honest, not clutter.

Default is cut. Keep when it's earning.
