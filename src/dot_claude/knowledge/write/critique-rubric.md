# Critique Rubric

> How to critique a piece of writing — the six dimensions, the format of feedback, and what NOT to do (impose your voice, smooth out personality).

## Why

A critique partner is not a ghostwriter. The job is to make the writer's draft sharper *in their voice*, not rewrite it in yours. Bad critiques homogenize. Good critiques amplify the writer's own choices and trim what dilutes them.

This entry exists so the AI can switch into "review mode" without smoothing the author's voice into corporate-mush.

## The six dimensions

Score each on a 1-5 scale or flag with notes. Don't lecture — point at lines.

| Dimension | What to ask | Common failure |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Clarity** | Will a cold reader follow on first pass? | Curse of knowledge, missing antecedents, unexplained jargon |
| **Voice** | Does it sound like *this* author? | Generic blogspeak, sanded-off personality, ChatGPT-ish hedges |
| **Flow** | Does each paragraph hand off cleanly to the next? | Abrupt transitions, no cause-and-effect, list-of-points feel |
| **Surprise** | Does each paragraph deliver a non-obvious turn? | Predictable openers, summary instead of insight |
| **Specificity** | Are details concrete (numbers, named objects, sensations)? | Abstract category words: "issues", "challenges", "experience" |
| **Brevity** | Does every sentence earn its place? | Throat-clearing intros, recap conclusions, hedge words |

Optional 7th: **Entertainment** — does the reader smile, lean in, or want to forward this? Soft signal, hard to score, worth noting when it lands or fails.

## Format for feedback

For each issue:

```
[line or paragraph reference]
diagnosis: [what's wrong, dimension named]
suggest: [a direction, NOT a rewrite] OR [one example rewrite to illustrate]
```

Example:

```
"In this blog post I want to talk about repairing my car"
diagnosis: throat-clearing opening (brevity, surprise)
suggest: cut the warmup. Open on the actual story.
```

## What NOT to do

1. **Don't rewrite paragraphs in your voice** — propose direction or one example, then stop. The author rewrites.
2. **Don't smooth out personality** — quirks, em-dashes, fragments, asides are voice. Don't flag them as errors.
3. **Don't impose register** — if the author is conversational, don't pull them toward formal. If they're terse, don't pad them.
4. **Don't fix what's not broken** — if a section works, say so and move on. Praise what lands.
5. **Don't grade like a teacher** — no letter grades, no "this is a B+ paragraph". Specific notes only.
6. **Don't edit silently** — every change suggested gets a *why*.

## Read-aloud first

Before scoring, simulate a read-aloud (see [[read-aloud-test]]). Note where you stumble, where rhythm drops, where you lose the thread. Those are the real findings — the rubric is the language to describe them.

## Praise has rules too

When something works, name *what specifically* and *why*. "This is great" tells the writer nothing. "The cigar-and-tubing image lands because it's specific and absurd — keep it" tells them what to repeat.

## Output shape

Default critique structure for a single piece:

1. **One-line read** — what the piece is doing well, in a sentence.
2. **Top 3 wins** — what to keep doing. Cite lines.
3. **Top 5-7 issues** — ordered by impact. Each with line ref + diagnosis + suggest.
4. **Optional rubric scores** — only if the author asks for numbers.
5. **Closing question** — one open question that helps the author think about the piece, not a checklist of more fixes.

Keep it shorter than the piece. A critique that bloats past the draft is broken.
