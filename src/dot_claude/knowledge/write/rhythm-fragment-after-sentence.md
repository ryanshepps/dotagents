# Rhythm: Fragment After Sentence

> A short fragment after a long sentence creates beat. Use sparingly to land punchlines, mark scene breaks, and signal the writer's reaction.

## Why

Sentence rhythm is felt, not analyzed. When every sentence is the same length, prose flattens into background noise — the reader's eye glides over without landing. A short fragment after a meaty sentence is a speed bump: it forces a micro-pause, lands the previous beat, and signals the writer is *in the room* with the reader.

Common fragments that do this work: `Cool.` `Fair enough.` `Anyway.` `Not great.` `Nope.` `Worth it.` Single words sometimes: `Brutal.`

## Pattern

```
[long descriptive sentence, often with a setup or stake].
[two-to-five-word fragment that reacts, judges, or pivots].
```

The fragment can be:
- **Reaction** — `Cool.` `Brutal.` `Fantastic.` (often ironic)
- **Judgment** — `Fair enough.` `Worth it.` `Not great.`
- **Pivot** — `Anyway.` `Moving on.` `Whatever.`
- **Concession** — `In fairness.` `To be clear.` `Granted.`

## Examples

**Without rhythm:**
> First time using a torque wrench, and I was so paranoid about under-tightening that I leaned right past the click and snapped the head clean off, and I had to add a few hours and a trip for an extractor kit to the project timeline.

One long flat run. The mistake and the consequence blur together.

**With rhythm:**
> First time using a torque wrench, and I was so paranoid about under-tightening that I leaned right past the click and snapped the head clean off. Cool. New skill unlocked: extracting a sheared bolt. Add a few hours and a trip for an extractor kit to the project timeline.

`Cool.` is the speed bump. It lands the snap, signals the writer's reaction (deadpan, ironic), and sets up the recovery. The same content reads twice as well.

## The dose

Use this sparingly. A fragment after every sentence becomes its own monotony — staccato exhausts the reader the same way long-flat-runs bore them. Rule of thumb: one fragment per 3-5 full sentences, placed at the moments that need landing.

## When NOT to use

- **Formal writing** — annual reports, legal docs, executive summaries. Fragments read as flippant.
- **Building tension** — if the paragraph is climbing toward a payoff, don't deflate it with a deadpan reaction halfway up. Save the fragment for the landing.
- **Explanation-heavy prose** — when you're teaching the reader something, rhythm matters less than clarity. Fragments interrupt the lesson.

## How to spot the opportunity

Read your draft aloud. Notice where you naturally pause but the page has no pause marker. That's where a fragment goes. If you read aloud and never pause for a breath, the prose is too uniform — find the moment that deserves a beat and put one there.
