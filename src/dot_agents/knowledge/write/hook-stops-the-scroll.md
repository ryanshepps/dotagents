# Hook Stops the Scroll

> First line of a social post must earn a scroll-stop. Treat it as a separate craft from the body — its only job is to make the reader pause and expand.

## Why

On a feed, the reader is moving. They are not deciding *whether to read your post* — they are deciding *whether to stop scrolling*. The hook is the only sentence that fights that motion. If it doesn't land, the rest of the post is invisible.

Distinct from [[lead-with-the-news]]: that rule orders information by importance for a reader who already opened the piece. The hook is upstream — it earns the open. A perfect lede behind a flat hook is unread.

## Pattern

Draft the body first. Then write the hook last, when you know what payload it must promise. Test it standalone:

- Hide the rest of the post. Read only the first line.
- Would *you* stop scrolling? If no, rewrite.
- Does it promise something specific? Vague hooks ("Some thoughts on writing") lose to specific ones ("I deleted 12,000 followers last month. Best decision I made all year.").

## Rules

1. **Pattern-interrupt.** Break the rhythm of the feed — a number, a contradiction, a hard claim, an admission.
2. **Curiosity gap.** Open a loop the body closes. Don't resolve in the hook.
3. **No warmup.** "I want to share a quick thought…" is a scroll-trigger, not a scroll-stop. See [[cut-warmup-openings]].
4. **One promise per hook.** Multiple hooks compete; one wins.

## Anti-patterns

- Apologies ("Sorry I haven't posted in a while…")
- Throat-clearing ("So I've been thinking about…")
- Vague abstraction ("Leadership is hard.")
- Front-loaded credentials ("As a 10-year founder…") — readers don't care yet.

## In critique

Cover the body. Ask the author: "Would *you* stop on this line?" If they hesitate, the hook needs rewriting before anything else in the post matters.
