# Avoid Hedging

> Cut "I think," "maybe," "kind of," "sort of." State the claim directly. When uncertainty is real, quantify it ("70% confident," "likely") instead of softening every sentence.

## Why

Hedging serves two functions: it signals genuine uncertainty, and it lets the writer dodge accountability for a claim. The first is honest. The second is noise. Most "I think" / "maybe" / "I feel like" hedges are the second — reflex disclaimers attached to claims the writer is actually willing to defend.

Reflex hedging trains the reader to discount everything you write. Every claim arrives wrapped, so the reader can't tell which ones you'd stake your reputation on and which are guesses. Cut the wrappers and the strong claims start landing.

## The hit list

Reflex hedges to cut by default:

- *I think* / *I believe* / *I feel like* (when stating a fact you're confident in)
- *maybe* / *perhaps* / *possibly* (when used as filler, not calibration)
- *kind of* / *sort of* / *a bit*
- *some would say* / *one could argue* (when *you* are the one arguing)
- *just* / *probably* (when adding nothing)

## When hedging earns its place

Honest uncertainty deserves honest language. The fix isn't to remove all hedges — it's to *calibrate* them.

- **Quantified uncertainty** — "I'd put this at 70% likely." "This holds in maybe half the cases I've seen." Better than "I think this might sometimes be true."
- **Marked confidence levels** — "Confident:" / "Speculating:" / "Hunch:" prefixes when mixing claims.
- **Diplomatic hedge** — "I think the migration is wrong" to a senior colleague is the right register. The hedge is social, not epistemic. Different rule.

## Examples

| Hedged | Direct |
|--------|--------|
| I think this is probably the right approach. | This is the right approach. |
| It seems like maybe we should consider rolling back. | We should roll back. |
| I feel like the data kind of suggests... | The data shows... |
| One could argue that this is a bug. | This is a bug. |

When the direct version feels too strong, the lesson is usually that the original claim wasn't well-formed — not that it needs more hedge.

## In critique mode

Flag every reflex hedge. Ask: "Are you genuinely uncertain, or padding?" If padding, cut. If uncertain, replace with a calibrated phrase.
