# Specificity Beats Abstraction

> Concrete details — numbers, brand names, physical sensations, named objects — make prose vivid and trustworthy. Abstractions slide off the reader.

## Why

Readers picture concrete nouns and verbs. They cannot picture "issues," "challenges," or "things went wrong." A specific detail does two jobs: paints the scene AND signals the writer was actually there. Abstractions signal the opposite — a generalization is what you write when you don't remember (or weren't paying attention).

For personal writing, specificity is the entire game. The reader is here for *your* version of the experience, not a Wikipedia summary of it.

## Pattern

Replace category words with specimens:

| Abstract | Concrete |
|----------|----------|
| It was expensive | $1500 quote |
| Cold weather | Bare hands on cold metal, sockets in snowbanks |
| It went wrong | Snapped the bolt head clean off |
| A common issue | The check valve gets sucked into the engine |
| I tried to find the leak | Stood in my driveway puffing a cigar into a length of tubing |

Numbers, brands, model years, place names, body sensations, sound effects, quoted dialogue, exact tools used — these are all upgrades over their category words.

## When abstraction is right

Use abstraction only when:
1. **Stakes are tiny** — "I went to the store" doesn't need the store name unless the store matters.
2. **Specificity would betray confidence/privacy** — anonymizing for legitimate reasons.
3. **You're framing a class, not an instance** — "Vacuum leaks can be anywhere" is a deliberate generalization that sets up the specific hunt.

Default to specific. Abstract on purpose.

## How to revise

Run a pass on a draft and circle every abstract noun: *issues, things, stuff, challenges, problems, situation, experience*. For each, ask: what was the actual instance? Replace.

Same for adjectives: *expensive, cold, hard, frustrating*. What was the dollar amount, the temperature, the specific failure mode, the moment you wanted to throw the wrench?

## Examples

**Generic:**
> The diagnosis was challenging and took several attempts before I figured it out.

**Specific:**
> First attempt: a cigar and a length of tubing. I stood in my driveway looking like I was performing some kind of automotive seance, getting lightheaded, seeing zero smoke escape.

The specific version is funnier, more visual, and earns the reader's trust because it could only be written by someone who was there.
