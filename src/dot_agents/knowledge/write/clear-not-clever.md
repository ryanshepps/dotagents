# Clear, Not Clever

> When clarity and cleverness compete, pick clarity. Wit costs comprehension at feed speed. Save the cleverness for posts where the reader is already engaged.

## Why

Distinct from [[cut-clutter]] (which removes filler) and [[just-say-it]] (which removes hedging). This rule is about the trap of *writing for your own amusement* — turning a phrase, landing a pun, being seen as smart — at the cost of the reader understanding you in two seconds.

On a feed, the reader is not impressed by your cleverness if they don't get the post on the first pass. They scroll. Cleverness only pays when the reader has already committed; on a hook line, it almost always costs.

Bush & Cole: **CLEAR beats CLEVER.** The writers who scale on social are the ones who optimize for "the reader gets it instantly."

## Pattern

For every line, ask: "Is this the clearest version, or the cleverest?"

- If both, ship.
- If only clever, rewrite for clear.
- If only clear, ship.

The hardest move is killing a line you're proud of. Do it anyway. The reader didn't write it; they don't share your attachment.

## Examples

| Clever (avoid) | Clear (ship) |
|---|---|
| "I've been bullish on writing since before bull markets had bulls." | "I've been writing online for 8 years." |
| "Engagement is a vanity metric dressed in marketing's Sunday best." | "Engagement is a vanity metric." |
| "The algo giveth, the algo taketh — but mostly it taketh." | "The algorithm is unpredictable. Don't optimize for it." |

The clear version always wins on a cold feed.

## When clever is right

- **You have an established audience** who reads everything you post. They'll work for the joke.
- **The cleverness IS the idea** — wordplay that exposes a real insight (rare).
- **In the body, after a clear hook has earned the read.** Reward the engaged reader; don't tax the skimmer.

## In critique

Flag any line where you smile at the wordplay before you understand the point. Tell the author: "I get the joke but not the idea. Pick one."
