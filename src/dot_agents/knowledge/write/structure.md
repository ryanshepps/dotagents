# Structure

How a piece is built — openings that earn attention, transitions that carry weight, evidence that lands. Use when outlining, drafting long-form, or fixing a piece that wanders.

## Entries (by priority)

- [[hook-stops-the-scroll]] (p1) — First line of a social post must earn a scroll-stop. Treat it as a separate craft from the body — its only job is to make the reader pause and expand.
- [[lead-with-the-news]] (p1) — Put the most important sentence first. Reader gets the point before deciding whether to keep reading. Background, caveats, and reasoning come after.
- [[one-idea-per-post]] (p1) — Each social post carries exactly one idea. One tweet = one claim. One thread = one argument. One LinkedIn post = one insight. Multiple ideas = no idea lands.
- [[skimmable-formatting]] (p1) — Social posts get skimmed on mobile. Short paragraphs, line breaks between thoughts, no walls of text. Readability on a phone screen is the constraint.
- [[specificity-beats-abstraction]] (p1) — Concrete details — numbers, brand names, physical sensations, named objects — make prose vivid and trustworthy. Abstractions slide off the reader.
- [[surprise-as-engine]] (p1) — Readers stay because each paragraph delivers something they didn't expect. Predictable prose loses attention even when it's correct.
- [[zero-click-content]] (p1) — Give the full payload inside the post. Don't tease and link out. Algorithms suppress off-platform links; readers reward in-feed value with engagement that compounds.
- [[clarity-over-detail]] (p2) — Trade 5% of detail for 20% of clarity. Move supporting nuance to appendices, footnotes, or follow-up sections so the main thread stays readable.
- [[cut-warmup-openings]] (p2) — Delete the throat-clearing intro. "In this post I want to discuss…" / "Recently I've been thinking about…" / "It's been a while since I posted…" — none of it is the piece. Open on the actual content.
- [[end-with-engagement-ask]] (p2) — On social, the close is often an explicit prompt for replies, not a fresh thought. Engagement signals beat aesthetic endings on algorithmic feeds — but the ask must feel earned, not bolted on.
- [[end-with-idea-not-summary]] (p2) — Don't recap. Don't write "In conclusion." Leave the reader on a fresh thought, image, or unresolved beat. Endings that summarize the piece they just read are filler.
- [[hook-pattern-library]] (p2) — Named hook templates — curiosity gap, contrarian, before/after, listicle, story, stat, question. Reach for the pattern that matches the payload when the opening line won't come.
- [[show-dont-tell]] (p2) — Render scenes through sensory detail and action. Telling states the conclusion ("she was tired"); showing gives the evidence ("she yawned"). Showing builds trust and lets the reader feel the experience.
- [[if-then-sentence-structure]] (p3) — Frame arguments as logical chains — "If A, then B" — to force precision, expose weak inferences, and let the reader check your reasoning step by step.
- [[named-copy-formulas]] (p3) — Direct-response copy has a library of named structural frames — AIDA, PAS, BAB, 4Us. Reach for one when stuck on how to sequence a persuasive piece.
- [[swipe-file-practice]] (p3) — Keep a running file of posts, emails, and copy that worked on you. Annotate why. Study the file before drafting in a similar shape — and use it to teach the eye, not to copy.
