# One Idea Per Post

> Each social post carries exactly one idea. One tweet = one claim. One thread = one argument. One LinkedIn post = one insight. Multiple ideas = no idea lands.

## Why

Readers extract one thing from a post — at most. If the post offers three, they take none, because the post stops being "about" anything. Ship 30 calls this the **atomic essay** principle: each piece is the smallest indivisible unit of value. You can stack atomic essays into a long-form piece later; you cannot extract atoms from a compound post.

The discipline also forces you to *know* your idea. If you can't state the one thing in a sentence, you're not ready to post.

## Pattern

Before drafting, write one sentence:

> *"This post argues that ____."*

If the blank takes more than 15 words, you have two ideas. Split into two posts.

While drafting, every line either supports the one idea or gets cut. Tangents that "are also interesting" become future posts.

## Test

After drafting, ask a friend (or model): "What's the one thing this post is saying?" If their answer doesn't match your one-sentence brief, the post is unfocused — even if every individual line is good.

## Why splitting wins on social

- **Algorithm:** two posts = two impressions, two chances at engagement.
- **Brand:** a feed of single-idea posts reads as a body of work. A feed of multi-idea posts reads as a journal.
- **Reuse:** atomic posts compose into threads, newsletters, essays. Compound posts decompose into nothing.

## Anti-patterns

- "And another thing I've been thinking about…" — that's a second post.
- Numbered lists where items don't share a unifying claim — that's a folder, not a post.
- Two-paragraph posts where paragraph 2 introduces a new frame — split.

## In critique

Underline the one idea. If you can't, tell the author: "I count two posts in here. Which is the one you want to publish today?"
