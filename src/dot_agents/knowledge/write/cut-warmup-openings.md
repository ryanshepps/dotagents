# Cut Warmup Openings

> Delete the throat-clearing intro. "In this post I want to discuss…" / "Recently I've been thinking about…" / "It's been a while since I posted…" — none of it is the piece. Open on the actual content.

## Why

Most first drafts begin with the writer warming up: announcing the topic, apologizing for the gap since the last post, explaining why they're writing this. None of it is content. It's the writer talking to themselves before they get going.

Readers feel the difference instantly. A warmup opening signals "skip ahead." A cold-open signals "this is happening."

Distinct from [[lead-with-the-news]]: that's about *which sentence* leads. This is about *cutting an entire paragraph* of preamble that shouldn't exist at all. They stack — apply both.

## The hit list

Common warmup patterns to delete:

- "In this post / article / blog, I want to talk about / discuss / explore..."
- "Recently I've been thinking about..."
- "It's been a while since I last posted..."
- "Before I get into [topic], let me give some background..."
- "I've always been interested in..."
- "There's been a lot of discussion lately about..."
- "[Topic] is something that..."
- "When I first decided to write this post..."
- "Hey everyone, hope you're doing well..."

Any opening that *announces the topic instead of starting it* is suspect.

## Pattern

Try this on every draft: delete the first paragraph. Read the new opening. Does the piece still work? If yes — and it almost always does — keep the cut.

Try it again. Delete the (new) first paragraph. Often still works. The actual story usually starts a paragraph or two in.

## Examples

**Warmup opening (cut):**
> I've wanted to get into car repairs for a long time, and recently I did something that I think is pretty cool. I'll keep this one brief — I just want to introduce this fun hobby I have to any internet visitors.

This is the writer telling you *what they're about to tell you*.

**Cold open:**
> I fixed my own car this winter. Not an oil change — I tore the intake manifold off my 2014 Cruze, hunted down a vacuum leak through a Canadian cold snap, and put the whole thing back together.

Same writer, same story, but the second opening *is the piece starting* instead of describing what's coming.

## When warmup earns its place

- **News pieces with required context** — "Earlier today, the company announced…" — but even this is content, not warmup.
- **Personal newsletters with a relationship hook** — some newsletters open with a quick "hey, family thing happened, here's why I'm late" because the reader signed up for that voice. Voice is content. Apologetic boilerplate is not.
- **Reframing intros** — sometimes a paragraph of context is necessary to subvert it. If the warmup-feeling intro is a setup for an immediate twist, it's earning its place.

If in doubt, cut and see if the piece misses anything. Usually it doesn't.

## In critique mode

Mark the first sentence the piece would still work without. That's the cut line. Tell the author: "the post starts at sentence 4." Don't rewrite — point at where the piece actually begins.
