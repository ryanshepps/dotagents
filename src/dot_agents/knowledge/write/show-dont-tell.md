# Show, Don't Tell

> Render scenes through sensory detail and action. Telling states the conclusion ("she was tired"); showing gives the evidence ("she yawned"). Showing builds trust and lets the reader feel the experience.

## Why

When the writer states a feeling or judgment, the reader has to take it on trust. When the writer renders the scene that *produces* the feeling, the reader arrives at the conclusion themselves — far more persuasive and far more memorable.

For personal essays especially: telling reads as summary; showing puts the reader in the moment with you. Most blog posts that "feel flat despite being clear" are over-told.

## Pattern

| Telling | Showing |
|---------|---------|
| She was tired. | She yawned. |
| She was hungry. | Her stomach rumbled. |
| It was cold. | I dropped a 10mm socket and watched it disappear into the snowbank. |
| The job was hard. | Bare hands on cold metal, warming up in the car between steps. |
| I felt embarrassed. | I stood in my driveway puffing a cigar into a length of tubing. |

The showing version always carries more information AND more voice.

## How to spot telling

Look for:

- **Adjectives describing internal state** — "frustrated," "embarrassed," "exciting," "exhausting"
- **Summary verbs** — "experienced," "encountered," "dealt with," "went through"
- **Outcome words without process** — "challenging," "difficult," "rewarding," "interesting"
- **"I felt..." constructions** — almost always replaceable with the action that produced the feeling

These are candidates for replacement. Not every one needs replacing — but each is worth questioning.

## How to fix

For each instance of telling, ask: *what specifically happened that made me feel/think/conclude that?* Replace the conclusion with the evidence.

If you can't recall a specific scene, the original telling line was probably an abstraction the writer never grounded. Either ground it or cut it.

## When telling is right

Showing every beat would bloat any piece into infinity. Tell when:

1. **Pacing demands compression** — "It took three weeks of cold mornings before I had the engine apart." You don't need a scene for each morning.
2. **The conclusion is the point, not the experience** — opinion pieces sometimes earn the right to state the position. (Earn it by showing elsewhere.)
3. **The reader needs orientation** — a single telling sentence to place them, then back to scene.

Default to showing for moments that matter. Tell to fast-forward through what doesn't.

## In critique mode

Highlight every adjective describing internal state and every "I felt / it was [adjective]" construction. For each, ask the author: what specifically happened? If they can produce a scene, the line should become the scene. If they can't, the line is filler.

Don't write the scene for them — they have it in their head. Just point at the gap.

## Boundary with abstraction

Show-don't-tell is the narrative cousin of [[specificity-beats-abstraction]]. Both push toward concrete detail. Difference: specificity targets *nouns and numbers* (`$1500`, `cocktail weenies`); show-don't-tell targets *scenes that produce feelings*. Same instinct, different tool.
