# Swipe File Practice

> Keep a running file of posts, emails, and copy that worked on you. Annotate why. Study the file before drafting in a similar shape — and use it to teach the eye, not to copy.

## Why

Copywriters have called it a "swipe file" since the 1920s — a folder of ads, letters, and headlines that moved readers. You don't swipe to plagiarize. You swipe to *recognize patterns*. After 100 examples, you start seeing the structures (curiosity gaps, before/afters, contrarian openers) as moves, not magic.

For social writing, a swipe file is the difference between "I don't know why that post worked" and "that's a contrarian hook + atomic essay + low-friction ask." Once you can name the moves, you can use them deliberately. See [[hook-pattern-library]] and [[named-copy-formulas]] — those frameworks come from swipe-file pattern recognition.

## Pattern

A working swipe file has three things per entry:

1. **The artifact.** Screenshot or copied text. Date and source.
2. **What stopped you.** "The hook had a specific number." "The story dropped me mid-action." Be specific.
3. **The pattern.** Name the move — even if the name is one you invented. Cross-reference to your library.

Tools don't matter — Notion, Apple Notes, Obsidian, a plain markdown file. The discipline is the daily-or-weekly *adding to it* and the periodic *re-reading*.

## How to use it

- **Before drafting** a hook for a contrarian post, read 5 contrarian hooks from your file. Don't copy — let the rhythm tune your ear.
- **When critiquing** your own post that flopped, find 3 swipe-file posts of the same shape that worked. What's different? Specificity? Cadence? Hook?
- **Quarterly review.** Re-read the file. Patterns you couldn't see at entry-time become obvious in retrospect.

## What goes in

- Hooks that made you stop scrolling.
- Posts that got you to reply (rare and high-signal).
- Subject lines that earned the open.
- Bios you immediately followed.
- Long-form pieces you finished when you "didn't have time."

What *doesn't* go in: viral posts you found through "top posts of the week" lists. The signal you're capturing is "this worked on me" — not "this worked on someone."

## Anti-pattern

Hoarding without annotation. A folder of 400 screenshots with no notes is a graveyard. Better: 40 entries with one sentence each on *why* they worked.

Also: copying directly. The swipe file teaches the move; the move belongs to many people. The execution must be yours.

## In critique

When you can't articulate why a piece works (or doesn't), it's a swipe-file gap. Tell the author: "Add 10 hooks of this shape to your file, then come back to this draft." The diagnosis is usually pattern recognition, not technique.
