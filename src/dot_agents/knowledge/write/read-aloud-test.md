# Read-Aloud Test

> Read every draft aloud before publishing. Wherever you stumble, the reader will too. Catches clutter, awkward rhythm, formal phrasings, and run-ons that the eye skims past.

## Why

The eye is forgiving. The mouth is not. Read silently and the brain auto-corrects clunky prose. Read aloud and every awkward construction trips the tongue.

This is the single most reliable diagnostic across writing advice. Zinsser, Perell, Sivers, Stephen King — different schools, same tool.

For critique mode: simulating a read-aloud means tracking sentence rhythm, breath pauses, and where the natural cadence breaks down.

## What it catches

| Stumble | Underlying issue |
|---------|------------------|
| Run out of breath | Sentence is too long, needs a split or a fragment |
| Tongue trips on consonant clusters | Word choice feels written, not spoken |
| Sentence sounds like a memo | Too formal — doesn't match author voice |
| Reading sounds robotic and even | Sentence-length variety missing |
| You read the same word twice and it stings | Echo / unintentional repetition |
| You hesitate at "however" or "moreover" | Stiff transition word doesn't fit voice |
| You mumble through a passive construction | Active voice would be clearer |

## Pattern

Final pass on every draft:

1. Read aloud, ideally to an empty room or to a willing person.
2. Mark every spot where you stumbled, hesitated, or wanted to skip.
3. For each marker, fix the cause — don't just smooth it. Ask: was the sentence too long, too formal, too clutter-heavy, too predictable?

Don't trust silent re-reading as a substitute. The brain is too good at filling in.

## Variations

- **Read at conversation speed** — not slow-recital pace. The point is to mimic how a reader's inner voice will sound.
- **Read to someone** — if a real person fidgets or zones out, you've found a slow patch.
- **Text-to-speech** — works for self-review when reading aloud is impractical. Robot voice exposes weird rhythm faster than your own.
- **Walk and read** — engages a different part of the brain; pace problems become physical.

## In critique mode

Without a mouth, simulate it: read each sentence at speaking pace in your head, tracking where breath would naturally fall. Flag:

- Sentences over ~25 words without a comma break
- Adjacent sentences with identical structure (subject-verb-object × 5 in a row)
- Words you'd never say in conversation ("utilize", "endeavor", "in regard to")
- Paragraph closes that land flat — no rhythm-mark, no beat

Note these as "sounds-like" issues, not grammatical errors. The fix is voice, not correctness.

## What this is not

The read-aloud test is *not* a license to write the way people actually talk. Real speech is full of "um," repetition, dead-ends, and filler. Written-as-spoken means *flowing like spoken* — natural rhythm, word choices that don't trip — without the actual disfluency.

Aim for: how you'd tell this story to a friend if you'd thought about it for a minute.
