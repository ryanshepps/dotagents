# Named Copy Formulas

> Direct-response copy has a library of named structural frames — AIDA, PAS, BAB, 4Us. Reach for one when stuck on how to sequence a persuasive piece.

## Why

Persuasive writing is older than the internet. Direct-response copywriters (Schwartz, Halbert, Hopkins, modern: Wiebe, Glaveski) named the structural patterns that consistently move readers from attention → action. The formulas aren't magic — they're checklists that make sure you didn't skip a beat the reader needs.

For social posts, landing pages, sales emails, and any piece designed to *change behavior* (not just inform), reaching for a named frame is faster than re-inventing structure each time.

## The core frames

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

The grand-daddy. Open with a hook (attention), build relevance (interest), make them want it (desire), tell them what to do (action). Works for everything from billboards to LinkedIn posts.

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solution

Name the reader's problem. Twist the knife — describe the pain in vivid detail. Then offer the solution. Highly effective in sales pages and persuasive threads. Use carefully; "agitate" can become manipulative.

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge

Show the reader's current state (Before), the desired state (After), and the path between (Bridge — your product / framework / idea). Particularly good for case studies and transformation stories.

### 4Us — Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-Specific

Diagnostic, not generative. Run a headline through 4Us: does it offer something useful, with urgency, uniquely, in specific terms? Fail any and rewrite.

### FAB — Features, Advantages, Benefits

Translate what something *is* (features) into what it *does* (advantages) into what the reader *gets* (benefits). The third column is what the reader cares about — most copy stops at features.

## Pattern

When stuck on the structure of a persuasive piece:

1. Identify the goal: inform? persuade? sell? transform?
2. Pick the frame that matches the goal (persuade → PAS; transform → BAB; sell → AIDA).
3. Outline against the frame's beats.
4. Draft.

## Anti-pattern

Stacking frames ("AIDA on the outside, PAS in the middle, ending with BAB"). One frame, executed cleanly. If a piece needs two frames, it's probably two pieces.

Also: don't apply persuasion frames to non-persuasive content. A reflective essay forced into AIDA reads as a sales pitch.

## In critique

When a persuasive piece "has the right ingredients but doesn't move," name the missing beat. Often it's the action (AIDA), the agitation (PAS), or the benefit translation (FAB). See [[hook-pattern-library]] for hook-specific frames.
