# End With the Engagement Ask

> On social, the close is often an explicit prompt for replies, not a fresh thought. Engagement signals beat aesthetic endings on algorithmic feeds — but the ask must feel earned, not bolted on.

## Why

Tension with [[end-with-idea-not-summary]] (long-form rule: don't recap, leave on a fresh beat). Both can be right; the medium decides.

- **Long-form (essay, newsletter):** the reader is committed. Ending on a fresh thought respects their attention.
- **Algorithmic feed (LinkedIn, X):** the algorithm rewards replies. A post with 100 likes and 30 replies travels farther than a post with 500 likes and 2 replies. The ask buys distribution.

A clean post that ends in silence wastes the engagement opportunity. A clean post that ends with a specific question generates the comment thread that compounds reach.

## Pattern

The good ask:

- **Specific.** "What's your version of this?" beats "Thoughts?"
- **Low-friction.** Answerable in one sentence. "What's the one tool you'd cut from your stack?" beats "How do you think about your tool stack philosophically?"
- **Connected to the post.** Asking about an unrelated topic feels random and tanks engagement.
- **Single ask.** Two questions = zero answers (people freeze).

## Examples

**Bad asks:**
- "Thoughts?" — too vague, no friction-reduction.
- "What do you think about leadership?" — too broad.
- "Agree?" — yes/no questions get no thread.
- "What did I miss? What would you add? What's your take?" — three questions, no answers.

**Good asks:**
- "What's the one habit you've kept for 3+ years?"
- "Who taught you to write? Tag them."
- "What did you wish someone told you in year one as a manager?"

## When to skip the ask

- **Story posts** where the ending lands emotionally. Tacking a question on cheapens it. See [[end-with-idea-not-summary]].
- **Pure-value posts** (tactical breakdowns, frameworks) where the value itself drives saves and shares. The ask can dilute.
- **Hot-take posts** where the controversy itself drives replies. Asking is redundant.

Rule of thumb: 60-70% of posts benefit from an ask; 30-40% land better in silence. Trust the medium and the post type.

## Anti-pattern

The bolt-on ask: "…anyway, what do you think?" tacked onto a post that doesn't naturally lead there. Readers feel the manipulation. Worse than no ask.

If the ask doesn't feel earned, the post wasn't designed for engagement — leave it clean and let the next post do the work.

## In critique

Read the last line. Does it ask a specific, low-friction, on-topic question — or is it filler? If filler, either delete (clean close) or rewrite (real ask). No middle ground.
