# Profile as Funnel

> Your bio is a positioning statement, not a description. Visitors decide in 5 seconds whether to follow. State who you help, how, and the proof. Cut everything else.

## Why

When someone likes a post, they often click the profile before following. They have 5 seconds. Your bio is the second hook — and unlike a post, it's static, so it pays compounding interest forever.

Most bios are descriptive ("software engineer · cat dad · writer · runner · views my own"). Descriptive is invisible. Strong bios are *positioning*: they answer "what do you do for me, the visitor, that I should follow you for?"

## Pattern

Three things, in order:

1. **Who you help.** "Founders." "First-time managers." "Backend engineers learning frontend."
2. **What you help them do.** "Hire their first 10 engineers." "Survive their first 90 days." "Ship full-stack without losing their mind."
3. **Proof you can.** A specific number, a credential, an outcome. "Built 3 eng teams from 0→25." "8 years at Stripe." "200 manager coaching calls."

Optional 4th: **what you post about.** Names the pillars (see [[content-pillars]]).

## Examples

**Descriptive (avoid):**
> Founder · ex-Google · writer · I post about tech and life · DMs open

Says nothing. Could be anyone.

**Positioning:**
> I help first-time founders hire their first 10 engineers without lighting cash on fire. Built 3 eng teams 0→25 (1 acquired, 1 IPO). Weekly posts on hiring, comp, and interview design.

A visitor in the target audience knows in 5 seconds: this account is for me, this person has done it, here's what I'll get.

## Mechanics by platform

- **X:** ~160 chars. Hardest constraint — every word matters.
- **LinkedIn headline:** ~220 chars. Plus the "About" section for the longer version.
- **Threads / Bluesky:** similar to X, slightly more space.
- **Substack:** the publication description does this work.

## Anti-patterns

- Inside jokes the visitor doesn't get yet ("Professional dragon-slayer 🐉").
- Generic credentials with no outcome ("ex-FAANG · founder · creator").
- List of personality traits ("dad · runner · INTJ · coffee enthusiast"). Save for the third paragraph of your About section if at all.
- Hedging ("trying to figure out how to…"). See [[avoid-hedging]] — even more costly in a bio.

## Revisit cadence

Every 6 months. Audience evolves, pillars sharpen, proof points accumulate. A bio from 2 years ago is almost always wrong.

## In critique

Hide everything but the bio. Ask: "Would you follow this person?" If the answer is "for what?", the positioning is missing.
